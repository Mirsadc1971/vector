Illinois Condominium Property Act: Complete Guide for Boards and Owners

📅 Published: January 15, 2025 📖 15 min read ✏️ By Manage369 Legal Team
Quick Summary: The Illinois Condominium Property Act (765 ILCS 605) is the primary law governing condominium associations in Illinois. This comprehensive guide covers board powers, owner rights, voting requirements, recent amendments, and compliance essentials for Chicago and North Shore condominium communities.

Understanding the Illinois Condominium Property Act

The Illinois Condominium Property Act, codified as 765 ILCS 605, serves as the cornerstone legislation for all condominium associations in Illinois. Whether you're a board member, unit owner, or property manager in Chicago or the North Shore suburbs, understanding this act is crucial for proper governance and compliance.

Since its enactment in 1963 and through numerous amendments, the Act has evolved to address modern challenges facing condominium communities, from virtual meetings to electric vehicle charging stations.

Key Components of the Illinois Condominium Property Act

1. Formation and Declaration Requirements

The Act establishes specific requirements for creating a condominium association:

  • Declaration Recording: Must be recorded with the county recorder and include detailed property descriptions
  • Percentage Interests: Clear allocation of ownership percentages for common elements
  • Initial Board Designation: Process for establishing the first board of directors
  • Bylaws Creation: Required governance documents that supplement the declaration

2. Board of Directors Powers and Duties

Under Section 18.4 of the Act, condominium boards have extensive powers and responsibilities:

Board Powers Include:

  • Collecting assessments and imposing late fees
  • Preparing annual budgets and maintaining reserves
  • Enforcing rules and regulations
  • Entering into contracts for maintenance and improvements
  • Imposing fines for rule violations (with proper notice)
  • Borrowing money for association purposes
  • Purchasing insurance for common elements

3. Unit Owner Rights and Protections

The Act provides numerous protections for unit owners:

  • Access to Records: Right to inspect association books and records (Section 19)
  • Meeting Participation: Right to attend and speak at board meetings
  • Election Rights: Equal voting rights based on percentage ownership
  • Due Process: Right to notice and hearing before fines are imposed
  • Reasonable Accommodations: Protection for disability-related modifications

Voting Requirements and Quorum Rules

Standard Voting Thresholds

The Act establishes different voting requirements for various actions:

  • Simple Majority (>50%): Most routine decisions and rule adoptions
  • Two-Thirds (67%): Major decisions like amending bylaws or significant capital improvements
  • Three-Quarters (75%): Amending the declaration or terminating the condominium

Quorum Requirements

Board Meetings: Typically a majority of board members (can be modified by bylaws)

Owner Meetings: 20% of unit owners for first attempt; 10% for subsequent attempts if properly noticed

Financial Management Requirements

Budget and Reserve Requirements

Section 9 of the Act mandates specific financial practices:

  • Annual Budget: Must be prepared and distributed to owners at least 30 days before adoption
  • Reserve Studies: Required every 3 years for associations with 100+ units
  • Reserve Funding: Minimum 10% of budget must go to reserves (with exceptions)
  • Financial Statements: Annual financial reports required for associations

Assessment Collection Powers

The Act provides associations with strong collection tools:

  • Late fees up to $25 or 10% of assessment (whichever is greater)
  • Ability to file liens for unpaid assessments
  • Foreclosure rights after 6 months of delinquency
  • Recovery of attorney fees in successful collection actions

Recent Amendments and Updates (2023-2025)

Important 2024 Changes:

  • Virtual Meeting Authorization: Permanent allowance for virtual board and owner meetings with proper notice
  • Electric Vehicle Charging: New provisions protecting owner rights to install EV charging stations
  • Emergency Powers: Expanded board authority during declared emergencies
  • Electronic Voting: Clarified rules for electronic voting and proxies

Common Violations and Compliance Issues

Frequent Compliance Failures

Based on our experience managing 50+ condominium associations in Chicago and the North Shore, these are the most common violations:

  1. Improper Meeting Notice: Failing to provide required 10-48 hour notice for board meetings
  2. Closed Session Violations: Discussing non-permitted topics in executive session
  3. Record Access Denial: Improperly restricting owner access to association records
  4. Budget Timeline Violations: Not providing 30-day notice before budget adoption
  5. Election Irregularities: Failing to follow proper nomination and balloting procedures

Avoiding Legal Challenges

Best Practices for Compliance:

  • Maintain detailed meeting minutes and records
  • Follow notice requirements precisely
  • Document all board decisions thoroughly
  • Consult legal counsel for major decisions
  • Conduct regular bylaw and declaration reviews
  • Provide proper due process for all violations

Special Provisions for Chicago Condominiums

Chicago-Specific Requirements

Condominiums in Chicago must also comply with additional city ordinances:

  • Life Safety Evaluations: Required building inspections and certifications
  • Energy Benchmarking: Annual energy use reporting for buildings over 50,000 sq ft
  • Recycling Programs: Mandatory recycling for buildings with 5+ units
  • Fair Housing Requirements: Additional protections beyond state law

Insurance Requirements Under the Act

Master Policy Requirements

Section 12 of the Act mandates specific insurance coverage:

  • Property Insurance: Full replacement cost coverage for common elements
  • General Liability: Minimum coverage amounts based on association size
  • Directors & Officers (D&O): Protection for board members (highly recommended)
  • Fidelity Bond: Coverage for employee dishonesty and theft

Unit Owner Insurance Responsibilities

Individual owners must maintain:

  • HO-6 coverage for personal property and improvements
  • Loss assessment coverage for special assessments
  • Personal liability protection

Dispute Resolution and Legal Remedies

Internal Dispute Resolution

The Act encourages resolution through:

  • Written complaint procedures
  • Board hearings with due process
  • Alternative dispute resolution (mediation/arbitration)
  • Internal appeals processes

Legal Action Options

Note: Unit owners can pursue legal action for Act violations, including:

  • Injunctive relief to stop violations
  • Mandamus actions to compel board action
  • Damages for financial harm
  • Attorney fee recovery in some cases

Professional Management and the Act

Property Manager Authority

Under the Act, property managers can:

  • Act as agent for the board within defined limits
  • Collect assessments and pay bills
  • Enforce rules with board authorization
  • Maintain records and provide required notices

Management Company Requirements

Professional management companies must:

  • Maintain appropriate licensing (Community Association Manager license)
  • Carry adequate insurance coverage
  • Provide financial transparency and regular reporting
  • Comply with all provisions of the Act

Future Amendments and Proposed Changes

Pending Legislative Updates

Several amendments are under consideration for 2025-2026:

  • Enhanced transparency requirements for board decisions
  • Streamlined foreclosure procedures for chronic delinquencies
  • Updated reserve study requirements
  • Climate resilience planning mandates
  • Expanded electronic communication options

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can a board make decisions without a meeting under the Illinois Condominium Property Act?

A: No, except for emergency situations. The Act requires board decisions to be made at properly noticed meetings where owners can observe (though not necessarily participate in) the decision-making process.

Q: What percentage of owners can call a special meeting?

A: Under Section 18.1, owners holding 20% of the votes can petition for a special meeting. The board must call the meeting within 30 days of receiving a valid petition.

Q: Are condominium boards required to have reserve funds?

A: Yes, Section 9(c) requires associations to budget for "reasonable reserves" for capital expenditures and deferred maintenance. Generally, this means at least 10% of the budget, though owners can vote to waive this requirement.

Q: Can unit owners sue board members individually?

A: Board members are generally protected by the business judgment rule when acting in good faith within their authority. However, they can be held personally liable for gross negligence, willful misconduct, or actions outside their authority.

Q: How long must associations keep records?

A: The Act requires maintaining records for at least 7 years. This includes financial records, contracts, meeting minutes, and correspondence. Some documents, like the declaration and bylaws, must be kept permanently.
